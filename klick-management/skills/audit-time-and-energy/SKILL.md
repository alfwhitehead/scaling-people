# Audit Time and Energy

This skill produces a personal time-and-energy audit — a structured look at what activities charge you and drain you, where your calendar is misaligned with your priorities, and what boundaries you need to install or enforce. The output is a concrete set of changes you can make to your schedule in the next two weeks.

Part of `manager-self` (Conclusion of *Scaling People*).

## Rubric

1. **Track good days and bad days before drawing conclusions.** Before prescribing changes, gather evidence. Keep a check-mark system on your calendar: mark each day as good or bad for at least four weeks. Then look for patterns — which activities, time-of-day configurations, or meeting loads correlate with the good weeks vs bad weeks. Conclusions drawn from less than a month of data are usually noise.

2. **Know your energy signature and protect it.** Identify when in the day you do your best deep thinking, and guard that window. Scheduling hard cognitive work in your low-energy window is one of the most common self-inflicted productivity injuries. Put the calendar blocks in before others can claim that time.

3. **Separate "hard to start" from "wrong person."** Tasks that stay on your list week after week fall into two categories: things that don't fit your normal operating rhythm (need a protected deep-work block) and things you're genuinely not the right person for (need to be delegated or handed off). Treating a delegation problem as a scheduling problem is a common error. Delegate early; ask for help before you're overwhelmed.

4. **Learn to disappoint people with your time — on purpose.** The urgent-but-less-important work (reviewing a doc by tomorrow, taking a meeting that should be an email) crowds out the important-but-not-urgent work. Establish a default: route same-day review requests toward end-of-week, block calendar time to honor the commitment, and break the cycle of impromptu meetings by doing the 15-minute async task proactively.

5. **Compartmentalize; design your calendar to help.** A hard 1:1 or a bad piece of news will derail your whole day if you have no buffer. Schedule transition time between difficult conversations. Place energy-giving activities (a walk, a meeting with someone who energizes you) strategically after draining ones. Not every hard thing belongs on the same day.

6. **Flip your strengths audit.** Your biggest strength often carries a flip-side weakness. Name the weakness explicitly — because understanding it is what lets you deploy the strength more cleanly. If your strength is operating independently, name the trap: you accumulate resentment when others don't volunteer to help, or you skip peer input even when you need it. Naming the weakness is the fix.

7. **Ask for help before the meltdown, not after.** Overwhelm is not a character flaw; it is information. Bring prioritization problems to your manager in 1:1s early — that's what the 1:1 is for. The earlier you name it, the more options you both have. Waiting until you're in crisis forecloses solutions that were available weeks earlier.

## Draft-mode intake

1. "Walk me through a recent bad week — what was on your plate and how did the days feel?"
2. "What tasks keep getting rolled forward on your to-do list, week after week?"
3. "What's the one boundary you keep meaning to set but haven't yet?"
4. "When in the day do you do your clearest thinking — and is that time actually protected on your calendar?"
5. "Is there anything you're doing regularly that someone on your team could own instead?"

## Source

*Scaling People* — Claire Hughes Johnson, Stripe Press 2023 — Conclusion ("You"), "Manage your time and energy."

Encoded heuristic: Managing your time and energy starts with diagnosis, not prescription. Track good and bad days over a month before making changes. Once you see patterns, install the simplest possible intervention: guard your productive hours, delegate what the wrong person is carrying, build transition buffers around hard conversations, and set the boundaries only you can set — no one else will protect them for you. The two highest-leverage moves are learning to push back on same-day time requests (you can honor the commitment later in the week and stay off the reactive treadmill) and asking for help before overwhelm becomes a crisis. In a fast-moving company, even significant mistakes are quickly overtaken by new events — reframe, dust off, and move on.
