# Plan Internal Comms

This skill produces an internal communications plan — what gets communicated, by whom, on what cadence, through which channels, and how that posture shifts in change or crisis. Use it when ad-hoc comms are no longer enough.

Part of `org-foundations-and-planning` (Chapter 2 of *Scaling People*).

## Mode

Ask the user which mode to run in. Default to `draft` if they don't specify.

- **`teach`** — Socratic walk-through (10-20 min). Use when the user is new to this artifact or wants to internalize the framework. Walk through every heuristic in the Rubric as a question; build the artifact incrementally; explain the "why" alongside the "what."
- **`draft`** — Targeted intake → first draft → refinement (5-10 min). Use when the user knows roughly what they want. Ask the 3-5 questions listed under "Draft-mode intake" below; produce a complete first draft; offer one round of refinement against the Rubric.
- **`polish`** — Critique-and-polish (2-5 min). Use when the user pastes an existing draft. Critique against every Rubric heuristic; rewrite to address gaps; explain what changed.

## Rubric

1. **Start investing before 150 people.** Dunbar's number — roughly 150 — is the practical trigger: once an org hits that size, it becomes impossible to know everyone's name and role by memory alone. By the time you reach 150 people, you should have an internal intranet, clear communication guidelines, policies for what information gets stored and through which channels, and a process for deprecating out-of-date content. If you're waiting until it feels broken, you've already waited too long.

2. **Internal comms is either an equalizer or a discriminating force.** Whether people have access to important context should not depend on how long they've been at the company or who they happen to know. A doubling org means half its employees weren't around for the last year's decisions. Good comms gives everyone the same context independent of tenure, seniority, or location. Bad comms creates a two-tier culture — insiders and everyone else.

3. **Trust is inversely proportional to hypocrisy.** Internal comms that exists to convince employees that everything is fine is propaganda, not communication. Good communication is timely, honest, and willing to acknowledge mistakes. People forgive mistakes; they lose trust when information is hidden, false, or misleading, or when leadership says something and does another.

4. **Four criteria for a sound comms program: complete, accessible, reliable, transparent.** Complete means people can find what they need to do their jobs. Accessible means it works regardless of tenure, geography, or language. Reliable means it's consistent — meetings start on time, updates go out at the same cadence. Transparent doesn't mean sharing everything — it means everyone knows what will be shared, when, and with whom, including what won't be shared (e.g., individual HR matters).

5. **Communicate important things at least three times, through different channels.** People have different preferences for processing information. To ensure critical information actually lands — key metrics, a structural change, a company priority — share it in an all-hands, on a dashboard, and in a written update. Repetition across channels is not redundancy; it's how you ensure coverage across a diverse workforce.

6. **Communicate more in crisis and times of change, not less.** When a crisis hits, the instinct is to go quiet until you have answers. Resist it. Communicating without a decision is better than silence. A personal message that acknowledges the situation and shows empathy buys trust and time. Communicating too soon creates anxiety; communicating too late creates anger. Consult peers to find the balance, but always err toward more communication, not less.

7. **Make company-wide meetings earn their place.** As orgs scale, all-hands meetings become less effective per person-minute. Reserve them for emotional connection with leadership and for highlighting critical business results and achievements. If you can't invest the time to make them great, hold them less often. Poor-quality all-hands meetings are worse than no all-hands — attendance and trust both suffer.

8. **Invest in a writing culture as an infrastructure decision.** Writing is an equalizer: it gives context to people who weren't in the room, including people who haven't joined yet. Longform writing surfaces gaps in logic and forces precision. And it's efficient — you stop repeating yourself because the context already exists. The cost is document management discipline: distinguish evergreen from work-in-progress from one-time docs, maintain a strong search layer, and set clear expectations about when meeting notes go out and what they include.
