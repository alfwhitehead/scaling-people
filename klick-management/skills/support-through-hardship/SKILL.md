# Support Through Hardship

When something bad happens to an employee, the right response depends on what kind of bad thing it is. A one-time event, an ongoing hardship, and a situation with legal ramifications each call for different actions from you as a manager. This skill helps you distinguish the three cases, respond appropriately in each, and know when to escalate to HR or legal.

Part of `feedback-and-performance` (Chapter 5 of *Scaling People*).

## Rubric

1. **Case 1 — One-time event (death, miscarriage, severe injury, divorce, lawsuit): lead with empathy, ask what they need, cover the work.** Be empathetic and express an appropriate reaction first. Then ask the person what they need and how they want to be supported. Figure out what resources you can get them and how the team can temporarily take over their work. Do not ask about timelines or work handover immediately — usually people will let you know after a few days. The relationship you've built in 1:1s is what makes disclosure feel safe.

2. **Case 2 — Ongoing hardship (mental illness, sick family member, failing relationship, substance abuse): observe, share the observation, check in.** The employee may not tell you what's going on. Your job is to notice changes in behavior and check in. Share an observation, not an assumption: "I've noticed you seem a little down and distracted. I don't want to pry into anything private, but I'm worried something may be going on outside of work that's impacting things. Can I help?" Nine times out of ten, the person is waiting for you to notice. If they deny it and the behavior is serious, involve HR.

3. **Case 3 — Legal ramifications (sexual harassment, discrimination): know your obligations and stop the conversation if needed.** You may have legal obligations that override your personal discretion — in some jurisdictions, you are required to report disclosures of harassment. Before an employee discloses something to you in confidence, tell them: "I may or may not be able to commit to keeping this confidential," and remind them of your obligations and any anonymous reporting tools that exist. If you're already hearing something that has legal implications, involve HR immediately.

4. **Don't compartmentalize.** If something is clearly wrong, stop. Trust your intuition. Seek help. The worst mistake in these situations is to fail to act. Conversely, do not be alarmist and do not over-disclose to other managers. Keep personal details confidential.

5. **Balance empathy with business reality.** If someone needs to take time off, help them get the right resources — and be honest about how the work will get done while they're gone. You can be fully supportive of the person while also being transparent that you'll need to arrange coverage. These are not in conflict.

6. **Your obligations vary by situation and jurisdiction.** Know which disclosures require you to act — not just to want to. Involving HR early is almost always the right move when a situation has legal dimensions. Never manage a legal situation alone.

## Draft-mode intake

1. "Which case type is this — one-time event, ongoing hardship, or a situation with potential legal ramifications?"
2. "How did you find out — did the employee disclose it, or did you observe it?"
3. "What has your response been so far, and what do you still need to figure out?"
4. "Have you involved HR? Do you know whether there are legal obligations to report?"
5. "What does the employee need from you right now, and what does the team need?"

## Source

*Scaling People* — Claire Hughes Johnson, Stripe Press 2023 — Chapter 5, "Managing out, firing, and layoffs > When something bad happens to an employee" (subsections: A one-time event; An ongoing hardship; An employee's experience has legal ramifications).

Encoded heuristic: Horrible things happen, and every tragedy is unique. But there are three types of tough situations you may find yourself handling as a manager. For one-time events: be empathetic, ask what they need, cover the work, don't ask about timelines immediately. For ongoing hardship: be an observer, share an observation not an accusation, check in, and nine times out of ten the person is waiting for you to notice. For situations with legal ramifications: know your obligations, tell the employee upfront that you may not be able to keep things confidential, and involve HR immediately if the situation has legal dimensions. No matter the situation: don't compartmentalize, don't be alarmist, keep information confidential, and don't over-disclose. Apply the golden rule: treat others as you would like to be treated.
