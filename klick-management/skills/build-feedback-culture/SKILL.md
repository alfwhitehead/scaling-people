# Build Feedback Culture

This skill helps you diagnose your team's informal feedback culture and take concrete steps to strengthen it. Informal feedback — the one-off observations people offer in meetings and 1:1s — is the muscle that makes formal reviews feel safe. Without it, people go into review cycles not knowing where they stand, and the formal process feels destabilizing instead of constructive.

Part of `feedback-and-performance` (Chapter 5 of *Scaling People*).

## Rubric

1. **Model the behavior first.** The fastest way to build a feedback culture is to ask for feedback yourself — openly, repeatedly, and in multiple forums. "What could I have done differently in that meeting?" is stronger than "How did the meeting go?" The former makes clear you actively want the feedback; the latter is easy to deflect.

2. **Ask on different occasions and through different forums.** Ask explicitly in 1:1s, during or after meetings, over email, and in work sessions. Phrasing the ask as a request for something you can improve signals that you're genuinely interested, not just performing openness. Different people feel safe giving feedback in different contexts; vary yours.

3. **Normalize the practice publicly.** If someone gave you useful feedback in private, consider mentioning it at a team meeting without attributing it. "Someone gave me feedback on how I ran our last planning session and it made me think about…" sets the norm that giving and receiving feedback is welcome and unremarkable, not an event.

4. **Let the feedback sit.** When presented with feedback, the instinct is to problem-solve or explain. Both can sound defensive even when you don't mean it that way. Instead, repeat the feedback back to confirm you heard it accurately, then thank the person for sharing it. If you want to problem-solve, return to the topic at a later time.

5. **Follow up on the feedback.** Tell the person who gave you feedback whether and how you plan to act on it. Sometimes you'll decide it's valid but not a priority. Sometimes you'll dig in. Either way, the person needs to know they were heard — otherwise the loop never closes and they stop giving feedback.

6. **Praise publicly; give individual criticism privately.** Team-level feedback — honest assessments of how a project went, what the team learned, what to do differently — can and should be shared publicly. It builds a learning environment. Individual feedback belongs in private. Mixing these up erodes both trust and candor.

7. **Invest in informal feedback before the formal cycle.** If your team hasn't built the informal feedback muscle, the formal review process will feel like a shock. People will go into reviews not knowing where they stand, and managers will face pushback on assessments that weren't telegraphed. Informal feedback should make the review feel like a summary of conversations already had.

## Draft-mode intake

1. "How would you describe your team's current feedback culture — is it bidirectional, frequent, mostly one-directional, or largely absent?"
2. "What do you personally do to model asking for and receiving feedback?"
3. "Where does feedback tend to break down on your team — in 1:1s, in team meetings, at review time?"
4. "What specific change would have the most impact on how feedback flows on your team right now?"
