# Design Leader Hiring Process

This skill produces a structured plan for a leadership hire: the stakeholder loop, the transparency timeline, the interview process, the deliberation format (feedback collection, not group decision), and the offer and close approach. Use it when hiring a director or above — any role where the hire's impact is immediately felt by the organization and where buy-in from the people affected is part of what makes the hire succeed.

Part of `hiring` (Chapter 3 of *Scaling People*).

## Rubric

1. **Treat the leadership hiring process as change management from day one.** Introducing a new leader to an organization is like introducing a foreign substance into the body — organ rejection is a real risk. The hiring process itself is pre-onboarding: how you communicate, whom you involve, and how you handle dissent sets the conditions for the new leader's success before they ever walk in the door.

2. **Begin with enough early enthusiasm to justify the broader process.** Screen the candidate with two or three key people first — other executives, critical team members, or a board member — before involving the wider stakeholder group. Leadership hires are expensive from a time and organizational headspace perspective. Confirm that there is genuine excitement before asking more people to invest.

3. **Identify and include the stakeholders who will be most affected — especially those most resistant.** Map the people who report to the new leader, those who will lose or gain scope, and the cross-functional partners who will need to work closely with them. Include those who might be resistant, not just those who are supportive. Their buy-in matters more to the new leader's success than the buy-in of advocates.

4. **Run the deliberation as a feedback-collection forum, not a decision-making forum.** At the start of the hiring committee meeting, make explicit that the hiring manager will make the final decision — the committee's role is to inform it. Ask questions, summarize what you have heard, flag remaining concerns, and close by stating your timeline. Then report back once a decision is made and explain your reasoning.

5. **The hiring manager owns the decision even when the group disagrees — but do your homework.** If you believe a candidate is right and others are skeptical, make the reference calls, bring in outside advisers, and assemble the evidence. If your homework and gut both say yes, sometimes you have to make the call. Be transparent about your reasoning, and explicitly ask for everyone's commitment and support.

6. **For internal hires, do not run a sham process — but do validate the decision externally if it strengthens it.** If there is genuinely only one internal candidate, skip the fake competition and communicate the promotion thoughtfully. If you want to validate the decision, meeting a small number of external candidates can demonstrate rigor without undermining the internal hire. Keep internal candidate identities confidential.

7. **Handle confidentiality with care throughout.** Candidates in senior roles at other companies are rightly paranoid about their job search becoming public. Keep calendars private, provide only high-level updates internally ("We have strong candidates and expect to share an update once an offer is accepted"), and be explicit with your team about when you can and cannot share more.

8. **For references and offers, delegate compensation negotiation.** The CEO or hiring manager should not be the one negotiating compensation — it starts the relationship on the wrong foot and creates the impression that the framework can be overridden by the right ask. Use an experienced recruiter, the CFO, or a compensation lead to handle the terms while the hiring manager focuses on the relationship and the close.

## Draft-mode intake

1. "What is the role, and is it new, a backfill, or a layering situation?"
2. "Who are the key stakeholders who need to be involved — direct reports, cross-functional partners, board members?"
3. "Is there anyone in the stakeholder group who is likely to be resistant, and why?"
4. "Is there a strong internal candidate, or is this exclusively an external search?"
5. "What level of confidentiality is required, and are there any current incumbents who need to be managed through this transition?"

## Artifact

**Format:** Markdown document with sections: Role context and rationale for opening, Stakeholder map (roles and their relationship to the hire), Transparency plan (who learns what and when, including the layering or replacement conversation if applicable), Screening and interview process (stages, interviewers, focus areas), Deliberation format (committee meeting agenda and facilitation guide), Disagreement handling plan, Reference and offer approach, Internal announcement plan.
**Default save path:** `./docs/management/leader-hiring-[role]-YYYY-MM-DD.md` in the user's cwd. Ask before saving; ask before overwriting if file exists.
**Template:** Inline — no shared template.

## Source

*Scaling People* — Claire Hughes Johnson, Stripe Press 2023 — Chapter 3, "Hiring > The hiring process for leadership" (including subsections: Transparency, Decision-making, Managing the leadership hiring process, Hiring internally, Interviewing, Hiring disagreements, References and offers).

Encoded heuristic: Leadership hiring is change management before it is recruiting. The goal is not just to find the right person but to build the organizational conditions for that person to succeed from their first day. Run the stakeholder loop early and broadly — include the skeptics, not just the advocates. When it comes to the deliberation, be explicit that the hiring manager decides: the committee's job is to inform, not to vote. If the organization disagrees, make the case with evidence. If the organization agrees and you have doubts, hold firm. The stronger your process and the more experienced your interviewers, the more confidence you can place in their collective signal.
