# Develop Key Relationships

This skill helps you map and strengthen the relationships that most determine your effectiveness as a manager — your own manager, peer managers across the organization, and founders or senior leaders above you. The output is a short relationship plan: what the relationship needs, what you'll do differently, and when.

Part of `manager-self` (Conclusion of *Scaling People*).

## Rubric

**Working with your own manager**

1. **Leverage goes both ways.** Your manager should get leverage from you (no surprises, clear status, proactive context-sharing) and you should get leverage from them (unblocking, resources, advocacy, development). If only one direction is working, the relationship is underperforming. Think about what you appreciate from your own reports — your manager almost certainly wants the same things from you.

2. **No surprises; early bad news.** The single most reliable way to damage the relationship with your manager is to deliver bad news late. Surface challenges, missed targets, and team risks in 1:1s as soon as they are visible — not once they become undeniable. If you would want to hear it early from a report, your manager does too.

3. **If the relationship needs feedback, give it constructively.** You are allowed to provide feedback upward. Do it the same way you'd want feedback delivered to you: specific, behavior-focused, paired with a suggestion for how you might work more effectively together. Frame it as a shared interest — you both win when the relationship works.

**Working with peer managers**

4. **Map your dependencies before a crisis creates them.** Don't wait for a blocked project to discover who your key stakeholders and partners are. Proactively identify teams whose work intersects yours, schedule 1:1s or join their meetings, and share your team's objectives. Relationships built before you need them are exponentially more useful than ones built in the middle of an escalation.

5. **Be a good peer, visibly.** Honor commitments, share information that would be useful to others, and bring issues to the peer directly before they escalate. Other leaders are among your best resources — for practicing hard conversations, for understanding what matters in the org, and for learning what's working elsewhere. Neglecting peer relationships while focusing on your direct team is a common and costly mistake.

**Working with founders**

6. **Check the fruit before you push the idea.** Founders are operating from first principles; experienced managers are operating from experience. That's a productive tension, but it means many good ideas will land too early. Treat unripe ideas as fruit in a bag: take them out periodically, test whether the organization is ready, and put them back if not. Some will ripen naturally; a few the founder will reach independently. Patience is not passivity — it's timing.

7. **Build the Camry, not the Escalade.** When you want to make a change even if the founders aren't convinced, start with the lightest possible version. Skip the acronyms and the heavy platform pitch; propose a pilot using tools the company already has. Define what success looks like and when you'll evaluate it. The goal is to demonstrate, not to argue.

8. **Learn the founders' values explicitly.** Understanding what your founders will and won't trade off is as important as understanding your own team's values. Ask them to articulate the trade-offs directly: What are they willing to give up for quality? For speed? For culture? Use offsite conversations and 1:1s to build this picture before a conflict makes it necessary.

## Draft-mode intake

1. "Which relationship are we focusing on — your manager, a peer, a founder, or something else?"
2. "What's working in this relationship right now, and what's feeling off?"
3. "When did you last proactively invest in this relationship outside of a task or a meeting you both needed to be in?"
4. "If this relationship were working at its best, what would that look like concretely?"
5. "Is there something you've been not saying to this person that might be quietly shaping the dynamic?"

## Source

*Scaling People* — Claire Hughes Johnson, Stripe Press 2023 — Conclusion ("You"), "Foster relationships."

Encoded heuristic: Careers at high-growth companies are shaped as much by the quality of your upward, sideways, and founder relationships as by your team's output. Each relationship type has a different dynamic: managing your manager means making leverage bidirectional and eliminating late surprises; managing peers means mapping dependencies before crises create them and being visibly reliable; managing founders means timing your ideas for organizational readiness, starting with the lightest possible intervention, and investing early in understanding their values and trade-offs. The unifying thread is the same as with your own reports — invest before you need it, be honest sooner than feels comfortable, and treat the relationship as a shared asset.
