# Clarify Management vs Leadership

This skill diagnoses a moment as a management situation (running the system that already exists) vs a leadership situation (changing direction or setting one), and recommends the appropriate response. The book's other strong nudge: when in doubt, come back to your operating system rather than improvising your way out.

Part of `operating-principles` (Chapter 1 of *Scaling People*).

## Mode

- **`teach`** — Socratic walk-through (10-20 min). Use when the user is new to this artifact or wants to internalize the framework. Walk through every heuristic in the Rubric as a question; build the artifact incrementally; explain the "why" alongside the "what."
- **`draft`** — Targeted intake → first draft → refinement (5-10 min). Use when the user knows roughly what they want. Ask the 3-5 questions listed under "Draft-mode intake" below; produce a complete first draft; offer one round of refinement against the Rubric.
- **`polish`** — Critique-and-polish (2-5 min). Use when the user pastes an existing draft. Critique against every Rubric heuristic; rewrite to address gaps; explain what changed.

## Rubric

1. **Management creates stability; leadership drives change.** Great managers run teams that do the actual building — defining goals, setting cadences, giving people a clear view of their performance and career. Great leaders set vision, make people uncomfortable with the ambition of the goal, and push teams to abandon the stable and familiar. Knowing which one is needed right now determines the appropriate response.

2. **Technical problem vs adaptive problem.** Technical problems have a known solution and an achievable resolution (you consistently miss a service-level agreement; you fix the process). Adaptive problems are continuously evolving — they're an infinite game (how to set product priorities against evolving user needs and increasing competition). Managers are superb at technical problems. Adaptive problems require leadership. If you're trying to find the perfect process for a situation that will never stabilize, you're mistaking a leadership problem for a management one.

3. **"Leadership is disappointing people at a rate they can absorb."** Management is about the mechanics — are the processes right, are the teams right, is the dashboard working? Leadership is about setting the spirit of the organization: the goal, the energy, the "we can do this" belief. The more senior you become, the more the work shifts from technical execution to adaptive change — and that means learning to be disagreeable and demand more, even when it's uncomfortable.

4. **Less experienced people need more management; more experienced people need more leadership.** A new hire benefits from someone who can help them manage their day-to-day, develop skills, and think through tactics. A senior employee has already figured out how to get their work done — they're looking for a leader to set the overarching vision, pave the way for progress within broader systems, and push them to think and act differently.

5. **Come back to your operating system before you improvise.** When everything is changing, the operating system — your meeting cadences, your goal structure, your hiring and development rhythms — is what creates the stable foundation that makes change possible. Before you invent a new process or make an exception, ask: is there an existing forum, process, or cadence that should have caught this? "Think inside the box you constructed" is the principle; the box is your operating system.

6. **A common operating interface across your teams is a management superpower.** If every team you run has the same core elements — clear missions, stated goals, metrics, meeting structures, weekly and quarterly cadences — you can switch contexts seamlessly. You're not reinventing the approach for each team; you're applying the same system and customizing at the edges.

## Draft-mode intake

1. "What's the situation in one sentence?"
2. "Is the existing system unclear to people, or is the system itself wrong?"
3. "What would 'running the play' look like here?"
4. "What would 'changing the play' look like here?"

## Source

*Scaling People* — Claire Hughes Johnson, Stripe Press 2023 — Chapter 1, "3. Distinguish between management and leadership" and "4. Come back to your operating system."

Encoded heuristic: Management and leadership are not the same thing. Management is human-centric execution — setting goals, running cadences, developing people. Leadership is adaptive — setting vision, making people uncomfortable with ambition, driving change at the cost of stability. The technical/adaptive distinction (from Heifetz et al.) is the sharpest diagnostic: if the problem has a known resolution, manage it; if it will keep evolving, lead it. When in doubt, don't improvise — return to your operating system. The operating system (missions, goals, metrics, cadences) is what makes it safe to take risks and break the rhythm, because there is a rhythm to break.
