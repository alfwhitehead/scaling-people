# Manage Managers

Managing managers requires a different mindset than managing individual contributors. The job shifts from being a captain to being a colonel: you stay involved enough to unblock and coach, but you're no longer doing everyone's job for them. Your 1:1s change from solving technical problems to coaching adaptive ones. Your value is no longer your domain expertise — it's your availability as a thinking partner, sounding board, and unblocker.

Part of `feedback-and-performance` (Chapter 5 of *Scaling People*).

## Rubric

1. **Move from captain to colonel.** As a manager of managers, your job is no longer to be in the weeds with each team's work. You need a system that keeps you informed enough to unblock and coach your managers, but not so involved that you're doing their jobs. The transition from managing to leading — setting vision, goals, and metrics rather than solving every tactical problem — is the hardest mental shift to make.

2. **Teach managers how to steer the fishing boat, not just how to fish.** When a manager brings you a management problem, your first instinct will be to solve it. Instead, ask: "What are you going to do?" Use the Five Whys to help them get to the root of the issue. Most managers know the answer — you have to ask the question. Coach them toward their own solution rather than providing yours.

3. **Manager 1:1s are about adaptive problems, not technical ones.** Individual contributor 1:1 topics include: "Where is my time going? How do I solve X? What should I prioritize?" Manager 1:1 topics shift to: "What is success in a year? Are we making progress against our long-term goals? Do we have the right systems? Are my instincts right on X? Do I have the right team?" Know the difference and steer the conversation accordingly.

4. **Check in on how they're doing before diving into work.** Whether the other person proposes it or not, start 1:1s by asking how someone is doing — genuinely. Then agree on the most important item and talk about it first, even if it takes the whole meeting. The heavy, important thing is often disclosed in the last five minutes if you don't create the space for it earlier.

5. **When you're not the expert, your value is different — name it.** Once you're managing managers, you'll frequently be managing teams in domains where you haven't done the day-to-day work. Your value-add shifts to strategy, communication, problem-solving, management coaching, navigation, and connections — not domain expertise. Set this expectation explicitly in your "Working with Me" document or an early 1:1.

6. **Find them the resources they need, including outside expertise.** If you don't have the expertise a manager needs to succeed in their domain, your job is to find it for them — an outside mentor, a more experienced colleague, relevant training. Being honest about what you can and can't offer builds trust rather than eroding it.

7. **Spend time on the ground.** You may not have managed your way up through every team you now oversee. Dedicate time to doing the team's work directly — even just two hours observing or participating in operations. You'll learn more about the team's real situation in two hours of direct exposure than from many presentations. Share your observations with the manager.
