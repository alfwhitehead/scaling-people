# Run Hypothesis Coaching

This skill walks you through hypothesis-based coaching: treating your management intuition like a scientific hypothesis, gathering just enough data to test it, and sharing the observation directly with your report as a collaborative explorer rather than a judge. It is the everyday coaching method you reach for before a pattern becomes a formal performance issue.

Part of `feedback-and-performance` (Chapter 5 of *Scaling People*).

## Mode

- **`teach`** — Socratic walk-through (10-20 min). Use when the user is new to this artifact or wants to internalize the framework. Walk through every heuristic in the Rubric as a question; build the artifact incrementally; explain the "why" alongside the "what."
- **`draft`** — Targeted intake → first draft → refinement (5-10 min). Use when the user knows roughly what they want. Ask the 3-5 questions listed under "Draft-mode intake" below; produce a complete first draft; offer one round of refinement against the Rubric.
- **`polish`** — Critique-and-polish (2-5 min). Use when the user pastes an existing draft. Critique against every Rubric heuristic; rewrite to address gaps; explain what changed.

## Rubric

1. **Intuition is a hypothesis, not a judgment.** An intuition is a pattern you've noticed but haven't yet confirmed. Framing it as "I have a theory that…" rather than "You are…" changes the entire tone of the conversation. Your report experiences you as an ally testing an idea together, not an authority pronouncing a verdict.

2. **You have more data than you think.** Before deciding you need more evidence, inventory what you already know: the person's role, their working style, the organizational context, recent work outputs, and any feedback they've volunteered about themselves. A manager who thinks they have three data points often has ten or fifteen when they stop to count.

3. **Gather data, form a hypothesis, test your hypothesis — in order.** The three steps are sequential and can be repeated. Skipping straight to testing before you have a coherent hypothesis produces unfocused conversations. Lingering indefinitely in the gathering phase delays the coaching that could help the person improve.

4. **Test by sharing directly with the report.** The best way to test a hypothesis is to say it aloud to the person: "I'm testing a hypothesis — let me know whether it feels right to you." Frame it as an observation about behavior and impact, not a judgment of character. "I have a theory that you're not getting key information to the right stakeholders" is testable. "You're a bad communicator" is a verdict.

5. **Self-awareness gaps change the approach.** If the person's self-description consistently contradicts what you and others observe, you have a self-awareness gap, not just a skill gap. The coaching response shifts: you need more data, more concrete examples, and possibly direct observation (joining a meeting, reviewing work product) before the hypothesis can land.

6. **File away the things they say about themselves.** When a report volunteers a hypothesis about their own behavior — "I tend to get too deep into problems" — that is a coaching gift. Write it down. Return to it at the right moment; it becomes the foundation of a conversation the person is already ready to have.

7. **Act early rather than waiting to be certain.** Hypothesis-based coaching lets you act quickly instead of relying on a long accumulation of evidence. Posit something and start talking. The sooner you and your report identify an improvement area together, the sooner you can make changes.

## Draft-mode intake

1. "Who is the report, and what behavior or pattern have you been noticing?"
2. "What data do you already have — role context, past feedback, recent work, things they've said about themselves?"
3. "What's your current hypothesis — what root cause or pattern does the data suggest?"
4. "Have you tested this yet? What was the response, or why haven't you raised it?"
5. "What outcome are you hoping for from the coaching conversation?"

## Source

*Scaling People* — Claire Hughes Johnson, Stripe Press 2023 — Chapter 5, "Hypothesis-based coaching" (subsections: Gather data, Form a hypothesis, Test your hypothesis).

Encoded heuristic: An intuition is just a hypothesis. You observe a few examples of a phenomenon and think, "I wonder if there's a pattern here." Like a scientist, you figure out a way to test your theory and collect more data. Managers often wait too long to share their hypotheses because they think it means they're judging their people — but good judgment is the manager's job. Hypothesis-based coaching is about saying the thing before you feel completely ready, but doing so in a way that the person experiences you as an ally in their journey to improve. The three steps — gather data, form a hypothesis, test your hypothesis — can be repeated, and the best test is to share the hypothesis directly with the report, framed as an observation about behavior and impact rather than a judgment about who they are as a person.
