# Manage High Performer

Managing a high performer is both the most rewarding and the most demanding part of the job. High performers pull more than their weight and exert more energy from everyone around them — including you. This skill helps you understand whether your high performer is a pusher or a puller, and what each type needs to stay challenged, recognized, and growing.

Part of `feedback-and-performance` (Chapter 5 of *Scaling People*).

## Rubric

1. **Identify whether they're a pusher or a puller.** Pushers are internally motivated, critical, and will push you on everything from work quality to their own promotion. Pullers take on everything, deliver reliably, and burn out in silence. Some people shift between the two. The management response differs: pushers need channels for their standards and help developing others; pullers need permission to say no and proactive recognition they won't seek themselves.

2. **Anticipate when the work will become boring — and say it.** High performers feel the strain of disengaging work before anyone else does. Don't hope they won't notice. Say the thing: "I'm worried we won't have enough interesting projects for you in six months." Start brainstorming with them now. If the right opportunity isn't on your team, help them find it elsewhere. Playing the person — putting their path first — builds the kind of loyalty that makes them more likely to come back.

3. **Create opportunity before they stop believing it exists.** Leadership roles, strategic projects, team-building work, architecture decisions, running the business review — these can be given without a promotion or a new title. Organizational structures can be adapted. The type of work matters more than the job title. Consult your career conversation notes and think about what experiences they need for the role they want in five years.

4. **Manage for potential, not just current performance.** Spend time identifying "interesting" talent — people with high upside and unusual skill combinations who haven't yet been unlocked. Your highest long-term return is often not from polishing the person who's already obviously great, but from figuring out what a high-potential person needs to break through.

5. **Run pre-exit interviews annually.** Identify your top 10 percent of talent and interview them once a year: "If you were going to leave, what would the reason be?" You'll learn about company pain points and gain personal feedback. At minimum, you'll be able to directly reassure them that you're aware of what needs to improve.

6. **Tell a high performer they won't be promoted — and own it.** Promoting too quickly is a real risk. If they're not yet ready, tell them explicitly what they're still missing and what needs to change. Be specific. Refer to the job ladder. Commit to the development path. If you've been giving mostly positive feedback and haven't built in the constructive feedback earlier, own that too: "I should have given you more feedback on X earlier — that's on me."

7. **Let go when there's no good opportunity on your team.** Stalling a high performer is always worse than losing them cleanly. If you can't create the right opportunity internally, help them land somewhere else in the organization. If there's truly no place at your company, open your network. Betting on the long term earns loyalty even through departures.

## Draft-mode intake

1. "Who is the high performer and are they more of a pusher or a puller — what signals that?"
2. "What's the current concern — disengagement, burnout, a promotion conversation, something else?"
3. "What's the opportunity landscape on your team right now — what can you offer in the next 6-12 months?"
4. "Have you had a pre-exit style conversation with them recently? What do you know about what would make them leave?"
5. "Is there a specific conversation you need to prepare for — a promotion 'not yet,' a career conversation, a retention pitch?"
