# Reflect on Career

This skill produces a personal career reflection document — an honest look at what you're learning, what energizes and depletes you, whether your current path matches your five-year shape, and what you might want to do differently. It's for you, not your manager.

Part of `manager-self` (Conclusion of *Scaling People*).

## Mode

Ask the user which mode to run in. Default to `draft` if they don't specify.

- **`teach`** — Socratic walk-through (10-20 min). Use when the user is new to this artifact or wants to internalize the framework. Walk through every heuristic in the Rubric as a question; build the artifact incrementally; explain the "why" alongside the "what."
- **`draft`** — Targeted intake → first draft → refinement (5-10 min). Use when the user knows roughly what they want. Ask the 3-5 questions listed under "Draft-mode intake" below; produce a complete first draft; offer one round of refinement against the Rubric.
- **`polish`** — Critique-and-polish (2-5 min). Use when the user pastes an existing draft. Critique against every Rubric heuristic; rewrite to address gaps; explain what changed.

## Rubric

1. **You are your own career coach.** No one else has the full picture of what's inside your head, heart, and gut. Don't wait for your manager or a mentor to bring you an opportunity — they don't have your inner data. Periodically take stock of whether you're learning what you want to learn and having the kind of impact you want to have. If the answer is no, that's signal worth acting on.

2. **Track aptitude and motivation separately.** Being good at something and enjoying it are not the same thing. Build a personal record of roles and projects you've tried, and for each one ask: Were you good at it? Did you enjoy it? Where did you have gaps? What skills did you acquire? Staying in a role because you're good at it, while it drains you, is a slow trap. Seeking a role you love but lack the aptitude for is a different kind of trap. You want both axes moving in the same direction.

3. **Use a 5-year shape, not a 5-year title.** Don't ask "What title do I want in five years?" Ask "What does the work look like — what decisions am I making, what functions am I leading, what kind of problems am I solving?" A shape is more durable than a title and more honest about what you're actually optimizing for. Test your current role against that shape every 6-12 months.

4. **Use short-cycle check-ins to stay on course.** Careers make sense in hindsight but feel incoherent in the present. Counter this with a three-interval rhythm: every 6 months, ask whether this role is teaching you what you want to learn; every 12 months, ask whether you need to change or augment the role to stay on your five-year path; every 18 months, ask whether the five-year aspiration itself is still right. Adjust before you drift too far.

5. **Chart your own course, not someone else's.** The biggest trap is chasing another person's version of success or staying in a role that doesn't use your strengths. Careers that look coherent from the outside may be wrong for you on the inside. Line up your head (aptitude), heart (passion), and gut (energy) before following prevailing wisdom or external pressure. The people who can help you see all three at once are rare — find them and keep them close.

## Draft-mode intake

1. "What time horizon are we reflecting on — the past year, a recent role change, or a longer view?"
2. "What have you been doing where you felt most capable and most energized at the same time?"
3. "Is there anything you're good at that you'd be relieved to stop doing?"
4. "What does the shape of your work look like in five years — not the title, but the actual work?"
5. "What's something you've been telling yourself about your career that might not actually be true?"

## Source

*Scaling People* — Claire Hughes Johnson, Stripe Press 2023 — Conclusion ("You"), "Consider your career."

Encoded heuristic: Careers rarely feel coherent in the present, even when they look obvious in hindsight. The remedy is self-authored, not externally provided: keep a running personal document that captures what you tried, whether you were good at it, whether you enjoyed it, and what it revealed about what you want to do next. Use a five-year shape (not a title) as your north star, then run short-cycle check-ins every 6, 12, and 18 months to test whether you're still on the right path and adjust early if you're not. The worst career outcome is not a wrong turn but unconscious drift — either chasing someone else's definition of success or staying in a role that doesn't use your strengths because no one has told you to leave.
