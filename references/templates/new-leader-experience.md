# New Leader Experience (NLE)
## Onboarding Plan for [Leader Name] — [Role]

*Use this template to plan and execute the onboarding of a new leader (director level and above). Fill each section before the leader's start date; update it as the program progresses. Reference: Scaling People, Ch. 3, "New leader onboarding," sidebar "New Leader Experience," sidebar "New leader onboarding: a direct report's perspective," and "Exercises and templates: New Leader Experience" and "Manager Transitions Guide."*

---

## Onboarding Support Team

*The success of the NLE depends on every role being filled and committed before day one.*

| Role | Person | Responsibilities |
|---|---|---|
| **Hiring manager** | | Primary accountability for the leader's success; daily/every-other-day standup for first 4 weeks; 360° feedback sponsor |
| **Spin-up buddy** | | Answers day-to-day tactical questions; guides through processes and norms; meets weekly for first 90 days |
| **Company guide** | | Peer mentor outside the team; safe space for "why do we do things this way?" questions; confidential sounding board |
| **People / HR partner** | | Weekly check-ins for first 3 months; Hogan assessment (or equivalent) debrief; 90-day feedback coordinator |
| **Onboarding point person** | | Logistics, scheduling, and checklist ownership; coordinates the onboarding support team |

## Pre-Start Week

*Goal: Leader arrives on day one with logistics handled and a first read of context, not zero context.*

**Recruiter actions (on offer acceptance):**
- [ ] Send leadership welcome/onboarding email including onboarding FAQ and upcoming company dates/vacations
- [ ] Confirm start date and first-week logistics

**Hiring manager actions (1 week before start):**
- [ ] Send introduction email to the company (or the leader's division) announcing the hire: who the person is, why you're excited, what they've been brought in to do, and one personal detail (photo, previous role, hobby)
- [ ] Share publicly available reading relevant to the role and company

**Onboarding point person actions (2 weeks before start):**
- [ ] Confirm any unique setup needs (equipment, access, security clearances)
- [ ] Send welcome email introducing the leader to their onboarding support team
- [ ] Pre-schedule all Week 1 and Week 2-4 meetings (see below)
- [ ] Prepare internal reading packet to send on day one

**What to include in the internal reading packet:**
- Company mission, operating principles, and founding documents
- Most recent company goals and priorities
- Division structure and key team documents
- Upcoming milestones and key dates
- Glossary of internal terms and abbreviations
- Links to key internal communication channels (Slack/Teams groups, email lists, etc.)

## First 30 Days

*Goal: Get oriented, absorb context, build relationships, identify the listening tour.*

### Week 1

*Leader joins company-wide onboarding sessions plus:*

| Meeting | With | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Onboarding welcome | People partner or onboarding point person | Share welcome doc, checklist, and reading packet |
| 1:1 kickoff | Hiring manager | Operating approach, early expectations, and communication preferences |
| Spin-up buddy intro | Spin-up buddy | Day-to-day logistics, norms, unwritten rules |
| Company guide intro | Company guide | Cultural context, safe space for questions |
| People partner intro | People partner | Development and feedback processes, Hogan assessment logistics |

**Hiring manager: schedule daily or every-other-day standups for the first 2-4 weeks.**
*(15 minutes. Purpose: quick answers, early impressions, listening vs. acting calibration.)*

### Weeks 2-4

*Priority meetings to schedule before week 2 begins:*

| Meeting | With | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Team deep dives | Each direct report (individual) | Understand current work, recent history, and team member's perspective |
| Skip-level intro | New skip-level reports (if applicable) | Signal accessibility; hear what the team wants the new leader to know |
| Finance business partner | Finance partner | Budget, headcount, and key financial context |
| Recruiting partner | Recruiting | Current open roles, pipeline, and hiring culture |
| Add to all-hands | Division all-hands | Visible presence; begin relationship with broader team |
| Manager transitions | New direct reports + their former managers | Transfer context, strengths, and development areas (see Manager Transitions structure below) |

**Week 4 checkpoint:**
- [ ] Hogan assessment (or equivalent) completed and debrief scheduled
- [ ] Leader has met every direct report in a 1:1 setting
- [ ] Hiring manager: brief informal check-in — what is the leader observing? What questions are emerging?

## Manager Transitions (for direct reports inheriting a new manager)

*Hold a three-person transition meeting for each direct report: current/former manager, new leader (if applicable), and direct report.*

**Transition meeting agenda:**
1. Direct report shares current work responsibilities and in-flight projects or goals; former manager adds any relevant context.
2. Direct report shares their view of their strengths and areas for development; former manager reinforces or adds to what they have discussed together.
3. Three-way discussion on how to support the direct report's development.
4. Direct report shares preferred ways of working for the new leader to hear.
5. All parties recap concrete next steps.

*Reference: Manager Transitions Guide in Scaling People, Ch. 3 appendix.*

## Days 31-60

*Goal: Move from absorbing information to forming early hypotheses and taking first actions.*

| Meeting | With | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Key cross-functional partners | [List names/roles] | Understand interfaces, current friction, and shared priorities |
| Other divisional leaders | [List names/roles] | Build peer relationships; understand company-level priorities from their perspective |
| Team immersion sessions | Broader team (groups or skip-levels) | Hear the team's perspective on priorities, challenges, and culture |
| Weekly people partner check-in | People partner | Observations, emerging feedback, coaching focus |

**Listening tour structure:**
For each key conversation, the leader should focus on gathering data, not providing conclusions. Share with each person:
- What you are trying to learn in this conversation
- That you are in listening mode and will share observations and initial hypotheses after 60-90 days

Ask consistently across the listening tour:
1. What is going well that I should protect?
2. What is not going well that I should understand?
3. What do you most want me to prioritize?
4. What do you most want me to know that you're not sure I'll hear otherwise?

**Decision-making in days 31-60:**
- Before taking any significant action, share the reasoning with the hiring manager and spin-up buddy.
- Use the shadow/reverse-shadow pattern for areas where the new leader is inheriting existing responsibilities: observe first, then take the lead with the prior owner shadowing.
- Document first impressions and early hypotheses in a shared doc with the hiring manager.

## Days 61-90

*Goal: Begin contributing actively; complete 90-day feedback cycle; establish "Working with Me" and personal development plan.*

| Action | Owner | Timing |
|---|---|---|
| Share first 90-day reflections with hiring manager | New leader | Week 12 |
| Create "Working with Me" document | New leader | By day 90 |
| Complete personal development plan | New leader + people partner | By day 90 |
| Join company-wide leadership and manager forums | Onboarding point person | Week 5-8 |
| 90-day 360° feedback interviews | People partner | Days 85-90 |
| 360° findings debrief | People partner + new leader | Day 90 or shortly after |
| First coaching session | Coach + new leader | After coach match (Week 9-12) |

## 90-Day Feedback Process

*Purpose: provide the new leader with early, honest input so course corrections are small.*

**Who the people partner interviews:**
- Each direct report
- Hiring manager
- 2-3 key cross-functional partners
- Spin-up buddy and company guide

**Consistent questions to ask each person:**
1. What is [leader name] doing well that you hope they continue?
2. What is one thing you would like to see them do more of, less of, or differently?
3. What, if anything, are you worried about?
4. Is there anything important that you don't think they know yet?

**Debrief format:**
Synthesize into themes. Present strengths, development areas, and 1-2 specific coaching commitments. Share with the leader and, with their permission, with the hiring manager.

## What the Existing Team Should Do

*Adapted from "New leader onboarding: a direct report's perspective" (Jorge Ortiz, cited in Scaling People, Ch. 3).*

1. **Commit to the new leader's success.** Everyone loses when a new leader fails. Make that commitment explicit to yourself and to the leader.

2. **Prioritize information transfer.** Ask what the leader's learning style is. Calibrate pace — fast enough to ramp them up, slow enough that they can absorb. Help them know what they need to learn now vs. in 30 days vs. in 90 days.

3. **Give data, not pre-processed conclusions.** Share facts and let the new leader form their own judgments. Save your hypotheses and opinions for after they've had time to absorb the facts, or share them only if asked.

4. **Use shadow/reverse-shadow for handoff areas.** For areas the new leader is taking over, agree explicitly: you operate, they shadow; then they operate, you shadow. Hold daily or every-other-day debriefs during this period.

5. **Model the company culture actively.** New leaders often don't know when they're not adapting to the culture — and few people will tell them. Lead by example on the behaviors that may be least natural to the new leader's prior environment.

6. **Give feedback quickly and often.** Timeliness matters more with new leaders than in any other context. Small early feedback prevents large late problems. If the direct relationship is too new for direct feedback, route it through a trusted peer or the hiring manager.

7. **Encourage adaptability.** Experienced leaders bring proven playbooks that were built for different environments. Help the new leader identify where their prior assumptions may not apply here.

8. **Map the org — formal and informal.** Draw the formal org chart. Explain scope, interfaces, and challenges. Map the informal org: key decision-makers, domain experts, high performers, challenging situations, and unwritten norms.

9. **Loan your social capital.** Broker introductions. Advocate for the new leader in small and large audiences. Set them up for early, quick wins. The fastest way to build their credibility is to transfer some of yours.

10. **Share stories and histories.** The current state of the company or division only makes sense in the context of how it got here. Tell the stories behind the products, teams, and decisions. This is often the most efficient path to genuine understanding.
